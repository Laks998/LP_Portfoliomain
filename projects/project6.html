VR & DUALISM

Designing the best VR environmnet and experience to evoke the feelng of Dualism

Are the mind and body truly separate?

This project set out to explore René Descartes’ theory of dualism — the idea that mind and body are distinct. Instead of approaching it as abstract philosophy, I used Virtual Reality (VR) to create an experience where people could feel that separation by combining insights from psychology (dissociation, out-of-body experiences, lucid dreams) with VR technology, the goal was to design an immersive environment that could trigger a sense of mind–body dualism.

The VR Environment

I designed a lift (elevator) scenario in Unity, structured to build tension and trigger heightened emotional states:

  1. Confined entry: participants began in a dimly lit elevator interior, seated in a matching real-world chair.
  2. Ascent: creaking sounds, groaning metal, and shifting lights simulated a rising, unstable lift.
  3. Exposure: the doors opened into a panoramic 360° cityscape, revealing extreme height.
  4. Loss of control: camera movement subtly shifted without user input, producing a sense of being moved externally.

This sequence was deliberately structured to create emotional intensity, a known trigger for dissociation from both literature and participant interviews.

Process

  1. Literature Review — studied dualism, dissociation, lucid dreaming, OBEs, VR illusions.
  2. User Interviews — 8 participants shared real experiences of dissociation, often tied to emotional highs or lows.
  3. Prototype Design — built elevator environment with matched chair setup and soundscape.
  4. Pilot Study — 2 participants tested the prototype. Feedback:
    • Height didn’t feel convincing.
    • Camera movement too static.
    • Visuals needed more realism.
  5. Iteration — refinements included:
    • Replaced shaft with a panoramic city view.
    • Added dynamic camera pans and slight tilts.
    • Improved lighting, textures, and sound design.
  6. Final Testing — 5 participants with self-reported fear of heights experienced the revised VR (2–3 minutes each) followed by post-session interviews.

Key Findings

  • Emotion-first design works: participants reported dissociation during moments of strongest emotional intensity.
  • Immersion depends on realism: graphics, sound, and camera dynamics combined to sustain presence.
  • External control deepened detachment: when the virtual body moved without input, participants felt less anchored.
  • Momentary body forgetting: multiple participants reported forgetting their real body for brief moments.
“I was so focused on the fear that I forgot where I actually was.” — Participant

What I Learned

  • VR is more than entertainment: it can act as a laboratory for abstract philosophical ideas.
  • Emotions are design levers: fear, awe, and shock can be intentionally triggered to induce altered states.
  • Iteration transforms immersion: pilot feedback on realism and height was crucial to success.
  • Ethical design is critical: exposure time was limited and participants were fully debriefed to ensure safety.

Impact

This project demonstrated how Virtual Reality can bridge philosophy, psychology, and design. By using emotional triggers, VR translated Cartesian Dualism from an abstract theory into a lived experience.

Future applications include:

  • Academic research — consciousness and altered states.
  • Therapeutic use — exposure therapy and dissociation studies.
  • Education — making complex philosophical theories tangible.
